Visualizing Two Practice Strategies

By Mike McGibbon, written 4.22.2026

We know that scores go up when students acquire important skills. But what is the optimal practice strategy? I've created two simulations, each representing a common approach to skill acquisition. In both, the goal is to "learn" 50 new questions.

Each circle represents a skill in a diverse pool, similar to what students encounter in tests like the SAT. Each turn represents an attempt to answer a question related to that skill. The probability of success is based on the color of the circle at that time, starting at 70% for new questions.

For example, if a circle's turns are unsuccessful twice in a row (-2), followed by a successful turn (+1), the running total is -1 (yellow). That means it will have a 60% chance of success on its next turn.

The first simulation represents targeted practice. The pool contains no mastered or too-hard questions at the start of the simulation. But most students prefer to take practice tests. These have a mix of questions, some of which have already been mastered, some of which are currently out of reach.

In the second simlution, the goal is still to master 50 questions, but this time the student is not specifically targeting the not-mastered, not-out-of-reach questions. Questions are selected randomly from the full pool, just as they are in a practice test. This particular student begins with 30 green checks (mastered) and 20 black circles (too hard).

It's painful for me to watch this one. Every pulsing green check and black ✕ is futile. Over time, it becomes harder and harder to find those questions that can still be converted. One forlorn green circle may wait for 80 turns…and that turn still might not be successful. The process also appears to be highly variable: I've seen as many as 3,947 turns and as few as 772.

I do think the second simulation reflects the approach of taking "test after test" approach very well. The student is answering more questions and is getting a higher percentage right (often ~75% vs ~65%)...but it doesn't matter, because so few of those questions are targeting skills that will actually affect their scores.

Meanwhile, the first student is only targeting high-yield questions. There are no black questions, and the green-check questions are never answered. Over time, the practice pool becomes smaller, which speeds up the process.

Questions

First, this is just a model. It's surprisingly hard to find any models of how improvement happens, especially ones that are interactive. There are theories and terms (interleaving, retrieval, etc), but no sense of how these ideas operate in real life and what the tradeoffs are. My intent is to start building bridges between theory and practice.

Why those percentages for those colors?

  • 70% is roughly the average percent correct on Mathchops, which also finds questions at a student's demonstrated ability level.
  • 60% is roughly the average percent correct my students have achieved on "redos" of missed questions on practice tests.
  • 100% is a bit optimistic for mastery, but if a student gets a question right twice in a row, she is very likely to get it right in the future.
  • 0% is too pessimistic (especially if you can guess via multiple choice), but for most students, there are questions that are impossible to get with their current skill set.

Why are you selecting randomly? Why not by category? I will explore this in a future simulation, but I have found it very difficult to select questions accurately by category when there are so many different and inter-related skills. My approach might be different in a smaller domain in which certain skills were clearly dependent on others. In broader, messier pools, it's very easy to waste time on questions that are too easy or hard simply because they fall in your pre-determined category.

But aren't some skills more or less related to others, which would drive up the probability of answering a question based on a similar skill correctly? This is my intuition also, but in practice, it is very difficult to find and model these relationships. Also, I didn't want this model to become too complicated (at least for now).

Don't tests select from a pool of skills, rather than from the same small pool every time? Yes. I will attempt to simulate this in the future.

What is the "size" of each skill? It's roughly "something a student could pick up in under five minutes through practice and instruction." For a student who understands fractions and ratios, that might be the skill of solving basic percent questions. For a student who is comfortable with the equations of parabolas and circles, that might be graphing basic ellipses.

Isn't your targeted practice scenario unrealistic? It's impossible to find a practice set without mastered or out-of-reach items while retaining high-value items. Yes, this is a fair critique. Students always have some small chance of getting an easy question wrong or a hard question right. Also, even the 50 questions we're targeting would be related in some way to skills the student has mastered to some degree. They aren't completely new. However, this is mostly a demonstration of the type of practice we should aspire to.

What about other important factors, like staying motivated over time and managing stress during a test? I'd like to model these in the future. They definitely play a role. But I wanted to keep the model simple, and I do think that skill mastery is the best predictor of performance.
